I used to like drawing since I was a child. I enjoyed manga and anime as well. In high school, I got to discover Hayao Miyazaki or Hideaki Anno’s works, this is where I understood how animation was dependent of the director. From that point, it made me aspire to become a creator and work in animation someday.

I went to art school, where I was specializing in fine arts. When I graduated, I thought again about my future.


"I figured I wanted to make animation. I decided to take the leap and to start training for it."

I had entered graduate school, but I dropped out and joined the industry from there.

For the birth of PUPARIA, I had been always impressed by animators, which motivated me to start in the industry. However, after 5 years working in animation, I started to feel as if I were losing myself.
No matter how hard your work, animation is something that is consumable. That’s what it is by nature of course.

However, it became harder and harder for me to cope with. I thought that pursuing that path would make me start hating my drawings.
I would start to hate even the simple act of drawing. I had reached my limit at the time. I thought I had to start from scratch. So in 2015,
I decided to fully stop drawing to make some time where I wouldn’t do anything. This is when I quit.

For about one year and a half, I didn’t do anything, without drawing at all. I just spent time reading and walking outside. During that time, ideas started to flow, I gradually wanted to make a work of my own. Nobody had asked for it, but I thought I’d give it a try and start.


"It was as if I wanted to leave a trace of my mindset of that time wandering around,
not doing anything. I thought of carving that mindset through the animation medium and dedicate myself to it. This led to PUPARIA."

PUPARIA comes from English’s “puparium”. It depicts the chrysalis or the pupa, meaning the shell of the larvae. Simply put, I had a strong feeling that the values shaping our world were gradually fading away.

I felt that the things we used to rely on and relate to were starting to collapse. I believe many people had that feeling worldwide,
even before coronavirus hit. The fact that we couldn’t be ourselves anymore somehow. At the same time we couldn’t see what the future was holding for us. I really had that feeling these past years.

From there, I didn’t want to make something patronizing. I wanted to make a story and a world that would wrap people from another angle.


"I aimed at expressing a feeling of being supported, I wanted to make something
like that."

For the graphic approach, I wanted for both the characters and backgrounds to be clear, without deception.


"I knew I was aiming to make something very abstract and difficult to understand."

So I thought it wasn’t a good thing to use difficult or blurry methods
of expression.

For a theme like this, I made sure for the surface to be as clearly drawn as possible. The characters are close to manga in my opinion. I like the symbolism you find in manga. Its symbols are so strong that sometimes manga can be considered to be “cheap”. I believe that is because what it expresses is so loud and clear, that sometimes the readers think it’s too much and consider it to be cheap.

On the contrary, I wanted to insist on the drawings to make sure my emotions were expressed through the characters. I wanted these emotions to show.


"I wanted to the characters’ expressions to feel alive to the viewer. It shouldn’t feel hand-drawn, but instead I wanted to feel life in their movements."

I believe that animation is also about the pictures. I enjoy animation when you see beauty in the pictures, so I wanted to set that in my own way.

The fact that it had to be made independently. Seeing PUPARIA,
I knew that nobody would want to make something like this. I didn’t want to annoy people by involving them into it. It was simply made from personal motivations. I really wanted to engrave my own emotions into that world.



There was no other solution than making it by myself. It didn’t matter if it would go ignored, as for me it was a necessary step in order to be able to go forward. So it really had to be an independent work.

Being all by myself was really hard. The workload was gigantic. There were days when I would clean drawings or just scan all day long. It was surreal to do this all alone. Having to do everything by myself and looking at all the time I was spending, I was really at my limit.



Looking at the pros of working alone on the other hand,
I enjoy understanding what a creator likes and what he’s aiming for in his work. It can be in the picture, the sounds or the music, it’s all engraved there. I really like that, regardless of the type of story,
I get a feeling of respect when I can picture what the creator wanted to express.


"Just bringing a project to the end is something respectable by itself."

I feel it’s harder and harder to achieve these days. Being able to express what you like without any shame, it’s something that needs courage. However I believe it’s a fundamental source of joy for humans. I wanted to see that, the anime works I used to see as a child were really showing these personal emotions. This is what had moved me, so this is the type of work I wanted to pursue.


"I really wanted to avoid lying and stay as honest as possible in what I wanted to achieve with my work, as I believe that’s what makes it beautiful."
